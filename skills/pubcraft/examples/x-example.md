# X (Twitter) — worked samples

Use these as reference shapes, not templates.

## Sample 1: Single post (240–270 chars)

### ❌ Generic AI draft

> Excited to share that we just hit a major milestone! 🚀 In today's fast-paced startup world, it's not just about building product — it's about delighting users. Grateful for the team. Onwards! 🙏

**Why this fails:** Hook-less. Banned tricolon ("not just X — it's Y"). Empty signaling. Zero numbers. Emoji slop. Grok's sentiment layer flags this as low-information content.

### ✅ Revised

> Closed a customer Friday who came in via a comment I left on a Reddit thread in October. Thread had 12 upvotes. Comment had 3.
>
> Reddit referrals don't show up in dashboards but they convert at 4–5x our ad CTR.
>
> Stop ignoring the long tail.

**Why this works:** Specific (Friday, October, 12 upvotes, 3 upvotes, 4–5x), takes a position ("stop ignoring"), no link, 247 chars, designed to provoke replies (people will share their own Reddit-conversion stories — that's the 150x conversation weight at work).

## Sample 2: Long-form post (Premium, ~1,400 chars)

### Brief

- Audience: B2B SaaS founders.
- Format: long-form Premium post.
- Hook target: contrarian-but-defensible.

### Draft

> Everyone in B2B SaaS Twitter says "double down on what's working."
>
> I think that's wrong, and I have data.
>
> Last summer we had two channels working: cold email (~30% of pipeline) and LinkedIn organic (~25%). The other 45% was scattered.
>
> Conventional advice: kill the scattered, double the two winners.
>
> What we actually did was the opposite. We capped both winners at their current spend and ran a 90-day experiment on the bottom 5 channels. Two were dead. One was flat. Two — Reddit and a niche newsletter sponsorship — were doing 3x the per-dollar pipeline of cold email.
>
> The reason is mechanical. The "winners" were already saturated for our ICP. Marginal dollar #11K on LinkedIn was buying impressions for the same humans who'd already seen us. Marginal dollar #1 on Reddit was buying a fresh audience.
>
> "What's working" isn't a fact about a channel. It's a fact about a *channel × spend level × audience saturation* triple. Doubling pulls you up the saturation curve fast.
>
> The scaling rule we use now: when a channel's CAC starts climbing for two consecutive months at flat spend, it's saturated for the next dollar. Don't double — diversify.
>
> We pulled this from looking at our own quarterly cohort data. I'd be curious if larger-scale ops see the same pattern, or if at $5M+ ARR there's enough heterogeneity in any one channel that saturation just doesn't hit.

**Why this works:**

- Contrarian thesis in first three lines (works as standalone — the "see more" cutoff is around line 3).
- Specific numbers (30%, 25%, 45%, 5 channels, 3x, $5M+ ARR).
- Takes a position with a mechanism ("saturation curve" — gives the reader a transferable concept).
- Genuine open question to invite replies from larger operators (conversation weight).
- 1,403 chars.
- Em-dash count: 0 (intentional — original had 4; revised cut all of them).
- No "Not X, but Y."
- Falsifiable specifics: ~5 per 500 chars.

## Sample 3: Thread (4 tweets) — when to use

Threads only work in 2026 when the structure genuinely needs sequential beats. Most "ideas" should be a long-form post.

Good thread example (each tweet a distinct beat):

1. **Tweet 1 (standalone hook):** "I read every Show HN post that hit the front page in March 2026. 47 posts. Here's the pattern that separated front-page from buried:"
2. **Tweet 2 (the variable):** "Title length. Front-page posts averaged 51 chars. Buried posts averaged 73 chars. Bigger gap than upvote velocity, time of day, or topic."
3. **Tweet 3 (the why):** "The 80-char limit is misleading — HN's mods truncate aggressively in the right column. A 51-char title fits without ellipsis. A 73-char title gets cut at '...' which kills the click-through."
4. **Tweet 4 (the takeaway, no follow-CTA):** "If you're submitting a Show HN, ruthlessly cut your title. Test it on the right column at 1280px width before you post. The title format alone doubled my front-page rate over Q1."

**What this thread does right:** First tweet works as a standalone observation. Each subsequent tweet adds a distinct beat. Final tweet doesn't ask for follows. No emoji. Specific numbers throughout.
